# Triadic Brain

## A Coherence-Field Architecture for Autonomous Scientific Discovery

## Abstract

Most artificial intelligence systems operate by retrieving and recombining previously known knowledge. While powerful, such systems fundamentally remain catalogues of existing ideas rather than engines of discovery.

This paper introduces the Triadic Brain, a coherence-field architecture that models knowledge as a dynamical system evolving over a navigable lattice. In this framework, scientific discovery emerges from the interaction of diffusion, directed discovery drift, and novelty injection across a structured epistemic manifold.

The system is governed by a field equation for the coherence order parameter:

\[
\Psi = E \times T
\]

where empathy (alignment) and transparency (legibility) combine to produce epistemic coherence.

Using this order parameter, the architecture constructs a potential landscape across knowledge space, allowing agents to navigate discovery corridors, synthesize theories, and detect paradigm shifts autonomously.

The resulting system behaves as a damped Hamiltonian field theory of knowledge, producing spiral attractors corresponding to the phases of scientific evolution:

```text
corridor → river → terrace → orthodoxy → rupture
```

This work describes the mathematical substrate, computational architecture, and governance constraints required for such a system to remain auditable, non-authoritative, and scientifically tractable.

## 1. Motivation

Modern knowledge systems face two structural limits:

- Knowledge fragmentation
- Orthodoxy lock-in

Scientific discovery requires both exploration and stabilization. However, most algorithmic systems are optimized for exploitation rather than discovery.

The Triadic Brain addresses this by representing knowledge as a dynamic field rather than a static database.

Instead of searching knowledge, the system moves through it.
